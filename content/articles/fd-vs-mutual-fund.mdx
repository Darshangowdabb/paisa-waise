You have ₹10,000 sitting in your savings account. Your parents say put it in a Fixed Deposit. Your friends say mutual funds are better. Who's right?

Both are right — but for different situations. Here's how to decide.

## What is a Fixed Deposit?

An FD is a deposit you make with a bank for a fixed period at a guaranteed interest rate. Currently (2026), most major banks offer 6.5%–7.5% per year for 1–3 year FDs.

Your money is guaranteed. You'll get exactly what the bank promised, regardless of what happens to the economy.

## What is a Mutual Fund?

A mutual fund pools money from thousands of investors and invests it in stocks, bonds, or a mix. The returns are not guaranteed — they depend on market performance.

Equity mutual funds (stock-based) have historically returned 10%–15% annually over long periods in India, but can fall 20%–40% in bad years.

## The numbers comparison

Let's say you invest ₹10,000 for 10 years:

**FD at 7% per year (compounded quarterly):**
- Final value: ~₹20,097
- Gain: ₹10,097

**Nifty 50 Index Fund at 12% average return:**
- Final value: ~₹31,058
- Gain: ₹21,058

The mutual fund gives you **2x more money** — but came with volatility along the way.

## The tax difference — this matters

**FD interest** is fully taxable as income. If you're in the 30% tax bracket, your 7% FD effectively gives you ~4.9% post-tax.

**Mutual fund gains** are taxed differently:
- Long-term capital gains (held 1+ year) are taxed at 10% above ₹1 lakh
- Short-term (under 1 year) at 15%

For a long-term investor, mutual funds are more tax-efficient than FDs.

## When FD is better

Choose an FD when:
- You need the money within 1–2 years
- You absolutely cannot afford to lose any principal
- You're saving for a specific goal like a laptop or trip
- You're a senior citizen (banks offer higher FD rates for seniors)

## When Mutual Fund is better

Choose a mutual fund when:
- Your investment horizon is 5+ years
- You can tolerate short-term ups and downs
- You want to beat inflation meaningfully
- You're building long-term wealth

## The inflation reality

India's inflation has averaged around 5%–6% per year. An FD at 7% gives you a real return of just 1%–2% after inflation. An equity mutual fund at 12% gives you a real return of 6%–7% — that's actually building wealth.

## Our recommendation for ₹10,000

**If you need the money in 2 years:** Split it — put ₹5,000 in a 2-year FD and ₹5,000 in a liquid mutual fund (safer than equity, slightly better than savings account).

**If you don't need it for 5+ years:** Put it all in a Nifty 50 index fund via SIP (₹1,000/month over 10 months). Stop watching the market daily and let it grow.

The biggest mistake people make is treating ₹10,000 as "not enough to invest seriously." It absolutely is — especially if you're 18–25. Time is your biggest advantage. Don't waste it on a savings account earning 3.5%.
